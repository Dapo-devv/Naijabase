There is something deeply Nigerian about the road trip. Whether it is the Christmas exodus from Lagos to the village, the Eid movement north, or a simple weekend visit to family in another state, millions of Nigerians take to the highways every year. But between the excitement and the jollof rice waiting at the destination, there is real planning that needs to happen. Fuel costs, route safety, rest stops, and vehicle condition all matter. This guide covers the practical side of road travel in Nigeria in 2026.

## Estimate Your Fuel Cost Before You Leave

The biggest surprise cost on any road trip is fuel. A trip from Lagos to Abuja looks straightforward on the map — about 900 kilometers — but the actual cost depends heavily on your vehicle's fuel efficiency. A sedan doing 12 km per liter will use roughly 75 liters of fuel one way. At ₦650 per liter, that is nearly ₦49,000 in fuel alone, before you even think about the return trip.

## Plan Your Stops

Driving more than 200 kilometers without a break is dangerous anywhere, but it is especially risky on Nigerian highways where road conditions, livestock crossings, and night driving all add risk. Plan to stop every 200 kilometers. Identify towns with decent fuel stations and food along your route ahead of time. Popular corridors like Lagos-Ibadan expressway, the Lokoja-Abuja road, and the Enugu-Port Harcourt route all have known rest points that experienced travelers rely on.

## Check Your Vehicle

This sounds obvious, but you would be surprised how many road trips end on the shoulder of the road with steam rising from the bonnet. Before any long trip, check your tires (including the spare), your oil, your brake fluid, and your coolant. If your vehicle has not been serviced in the last six months, do it before the trip, not after. A breakdown on a Nigerian highway is not just inconvenient — it can be dangerous.

## Travel During Daylight

Night driving on Nigerian highways is a different category of risk. Poor lighting, abandoned vehicles, livestock, and security concerns all multiply after sunset. If your trip is more than 400 kilometers, start early — by 6am if possible — so that you arrive before dark. If you cannot make it in daylight, break the journey and sleep overnight in a town along the way.

## Keep Emergency Contacts

Save the numbers of your state's FRSC (Federal Road Safety Corps) office, your insurance provider, and at least two family members who know your route and expected arrival time. Share your live location with someone you trust. These are small habits that cost nothing but can matter enormously if something goes wrong.
